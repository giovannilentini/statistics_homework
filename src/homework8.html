Homework 8: Deep Connections between Bernoulli Processes, Random Walks, and Combinatorial Structures

Due: November 8, 2025

Both the Bernoulli Process simulation of the Law of Large Numbers (Homework 4) and the Random Walk / Binomial Convergence simulation (Homework 7) stem from the same probabilistic foundation: a sequence of independent trials, each resulting in either success or failure. Yet, they emphasize distinct perspectives of the same mathematical world — one focused on stability and long-term averages, the other on fluctuation, dispersion, and cumulative behavior.

From Simple Experiments to Profound Laws

The Bernoulli process is the elementary model of randomness: trials yielding 1 or 0. In the Law of Large Numbers (LLN), the interest lies in the long-run average: \[ \overline{X_n} = \frac{1}{n}\sum_{i=1}^n X_i \] As \( n \to \infty \), this average tends to \( p \). Randomness becomes regularity.

Conversely, the Random Walk looks at the cumulative deviation: \[ S_n = \sum_{i=1}^n Y_i, \quad Y_i \in \{+1, -1\} \] Here, we study diffusion. Just as a drop of ink spreads in water, the probability mass of the random walker spreads out. The "width" (standard deviation) grows with \( \sqrt{n} \).

Geometry of Probability: The Galton Board

Imagine a random walk as a journey through Pascal’s triangle. Over many steps, the discrete triangle smooths into the famous bell curve. The physical demonstration of this is the Galton Board (or Quincunx). Balls dropped through a grid of pegs bounce left or right (Bernoulli trials) at each level, accumulating at the bottom in a predictable Normal distribution.

A Computational Perspective

We can visualize this relationship algorithmically. The recursive generation of Pascal's Triangle directly mirrors the logic of a Random Walk step.

C Recursive Logic 
public static int BinomialCoeff(int n, int k) {
    // Base cases: Edges of the triangle are always 1
    if (k == 0 || k == n) return 1;

    // Recursive step: Entry is sum of the two above it
    // This mirrors the walker coming from either left (-1) or right (+1)
    return BinomialCoeff(n - 1, k - 1) + BinomialCoeff(n - 1, k);
}

Conclusion

The Law of Large Numbers shows how averages stabilize, while the Random Walk exposes the architecture of fluctuation. Both converge in the same deeper truth: that randomness obeys structure.
